In ENG 2003, I produced a formal technical memorandum and report that required translating engineering analysis into structured, professional prose for a non-specialist audience. Every paragraph was revised with the 7 C's framework to ensure clarity, coherence, and conciseness throughout.

Why I Selected This Piece

Technical writing is the most common form of professional engineering communication, and it is the one most engineers underestimate. I selected this piece because it reflects a fundamental shift in how I approach written work — from completing a task to communicating with a specific reader in mind. It demonstrates that I understand written communication as a strategic act, not merely a mechanical one.

What This Piece Says About My Development as a Communicator

This memo shows that I have learned to subordinate my own preferences to the reader's needs. I applied Conciseness by cutting explanations that served my understanding rather than the reader's, and I applied Coherence by ensuring each section built logically on the one before it. This discipline in writing is new for me and directly attributable to ENG 2003.

Did This Push Me Out of My Comfort Zone?

Yes. As a computer science student, my natural instinct is to be brief and functional. Formal report writing required me to balance precision with explanation, and to write with a professional tone that feels different from code comments or casual academic writing. Learning to write in register took active effort and multiple revisions.

Is This My Best Work? Why or Why Not?

This piece represents my best formal written work from ENG 2003. It is not perfect — I identified areas where my sentences were longer than necessary and where transitions between sections could have been smoother. However, it demonstrates a clear command of professional writing conventions and a genuine attempt to apply the 7 C's systematically, which makes it my strongest written piece from this course.

Part A · Overall Course Reflection (250+ words)

My Experience in ENG 2003

My overall experience in ENG 2003 was significantly more transformative than I anticipated. I enrolled expecting a course focused primarily on formats: how to write an email, how to structure a report, how to present a slide deck. What I encountered instead was a course that fundamentally reoriented how I think about communication as a professional and intellectual activity.

Three experiences shaped this shift most clearly. First, studying the 7 C's of communication gave me a practical checklist I now apply to everything I produce. I no longer ask "is this done?" but "is this clear, concise, complete, concrete, correct, coherent, and courteous?" Second, preparing and delivering the Astro pitch presentation showed me that audience analysis is not a theoretical exercise; it is the difference between a message that lands and one that is ignored. I had to ask what the audience needed to understand and what they needed to feel. Third, the slide critique activity taught me that visual communication is a language of its own, one that engineers must learn to speak if they want their ideas to be understood at a glance.

The course aligned with my expectations in terms of developing formal communication skills, but it exceeded them in terms of philosophical depth. I did not expect to engage seriously with the axioms of communication, which gave me a theoretical grounding for why communication matters beyond task completion. The axiom that resonated most was that one cannot not communicate. Once I understood this, I began noticing it everywhere: in how a financial poster builds trust before a word is read, in how a team's posture during a presentation signals confidence or uncertainty, and in how the structure of a report communicates the writer's respect for the reader's time.

The content I found most useful was audience analysis, because it is immediately applicable in my career as a financial consultant. Every client conversation requires me to calibrate my language, my formality, and my emotional tone to a specific person. ENG 2003 gave me language and frameworks for something I had been doing intuitively but could not articulate. The topic I found most intellectually interesting was multimodal communication, which opened my eyes to the reality that meaning is constructed simultaneously across visual, verbal, and nonverbal channels. This was both theoretically stimulating and practically valuable for the poster design work I do as a financial professional.

Part B · Personal Growth and Development (250+ words)

How I Grew as a Communicator

Before ENG 2003, I would describe my communication abilities as natural but unstructured. In my career as a financial consultant, I had developed strong interpersonal communication skills through necessity, because speaking to clients requires confidence, empathy, and adaptability. However, I lacked a theoretical framework for why certain approaches worked, and I had significant gaps in formal written communication and structured technical presentation.

The communication skill I improved most dramatically was professional writing. Through the technical memo and report assignments, I learned that effective writing is not about sophisticated language — it is about making every sentence serve the reader. I applied the Gibbs Reflective Cycle to my own drafts: I described what I wrote, evaluated what was unclear, analyzed why it was unclear, and then revised with a specific reader in mind. This process transformed my approach from writing to drafting and revising intentionally.

A second significant area of improvement was oral presentation. Preparing for the Astro pitch forced me to practise pacing, verbal clarity, and the relationship between what I said and what appeared on screen. I learned that spoken communication in a technical context is not a performance of information; it is a conversation with the audience, even when only one person is speaking. The axiom that communication is contextual helped me understand why the same content needed to be delivered differently to judges versus teammates.

A third area of growth was visual communication. Completing the slide critique activity, and designing the financial literacy poster professionally, trained me to treat visual elements as communicative choices rather than decorative ones. I now ask whether a layout directs the reader's eye appropriately and whether a colour choice signals trust or urgency. These are questions I did not think to ask before this course.

The skill I still need to develop is spontaneous oral communication, specifically speaking clearly and persuasively without prepared notes. While I am comfortable in planned presentations, impromptu responses in question-and-answer settings still expose gaps in my ability to structure thinking in real time. I plan to address this through deliberate practice of the PREP framework — Point, Reason, Example, Point — in everyday professional conversations.

In future engineering courses, ENG 2003 will change how I approach every group project, report, and presentation. I will no longer treat communication as a separate component of a task; it is integral to every task. In engineering practice, the best solution that cannot be communicated clearly is not the best solution in practice. This course gave me both the conviction and the tools to communicate with intention.
